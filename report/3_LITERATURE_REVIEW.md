# LITERATURE REVIEW

## Existing Robotic Arms: Autonomous vs. Teleoperation Limitations
The dichotomy between autonomous robotic manipulation and teleoperation has been extensively documented. Autonomous systems traditionally rely on structured environments where object poses are known apriori or confined to strict tolerances. While highly efficient for repetitive tasks, these systems exhibit brittle failure modes when confronted with environmental stochasticity. Conversely, teleoperation allows human operators to infer context and adapt to novel geometries. However, classical teleoperation interfaces, such as teach-pendants or haptic joysticks, are inherently non-intuitive and demand extensive specialized training, thereby limiting operational scalability.

## Vision-Based Manipulation
Recent advancements in Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) have catalyzed a paradigm shift in autonomous manipulation. Models such as YOLO (You Only Look Once) have demonstrated real-time object detection capabilities suitable for closed-loop robotic control. Integration of these visual pipelines with depth-sensing apparatuses enables 6D pose estimation, directly bridging the perception-action gap. Nevertheless, the computational overhead of continuous inference and the susceptibility of visual algorithms to occlusion and varying illumination remain persistent challenges. Motion planning frameworks, notably MoveIt, have been integrated to translate these visual inferences into collision-free joint-space trajectories, though they are heavily reliant on accurately tuned Inverse Kinematics (IK) solvers.

## Human-in-the-Loop Systems
Human-in-the-loop (HITL) robotics seeks to amalgamate human cognitive superiority with robotic physical endurance. Markerless pose estimation frameworks, such as Google's MediaPipe, have recently provided high-fidelity spatial tracking of human kinematics using standard RGB cameras. By directly mapping human skeletal landmarks to robotic joint spaces, intuitive teleoperation can be achieved without encumbering wearable sensors. The primary technical bottleneck in HITL systems remains the latency induced by network transmission (e.g., MQTT or WebRTC) and the subsequent translation of continuous human motion into discrete, safe motor commands for the manipulator.
